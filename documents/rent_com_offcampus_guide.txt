---

Where to start your search: Begin 4-6 months before your intended move-in date if you're moving in September. University towns have synchronized demand — most students want to move in at the start of the academic year, which means summer listings fill quickly. For January move-ins, start searching in October or November. The best units go to the most organized applicants.

---

Types of off-campus housing options for students:

Apartment buildings: Professional management, standardized leases, amenities like gyms and package rooms. Often more expensive than other options. Usually requires credit check and income verification.

Rented houses (multi-tenant): Often rented by room with a shared kitchen and living space. Can be cheaper per person. Lease arrangements vary — sometimes individual room leases, sometimes a single lease all roommates sign. Understand which type you're signing.

Basement or in-law suites: Rented from homeowners. Often more affordable, sometimes more flexible. Quality varies widely — inspect carefully for natural light, ventilation, and moisture issues. Basements can have mold and flooding issues that homeowners don't always disclose.

Student-specific housing complexes: Purpose-built near campus with student-oriented amenities. Often lease by the bed with individual liability, which protects you from a roommate not paying. Usually more expensive per square foot.

---

What "all bills included" usually means vs. what it actually means:

Marketing language is imprecise. "All bills included" in a listing may mean: water, trash, and gas only — with electricity and internet billed separately. Always ask for a written list of exactly which utilities are included. The safest phrasing to look for in the lease itself is an itemized list of included utilities, not a marketing summary.

---

The apartment tour: what to do besides look at the space

Talk to current or former tenants if possible. Knock on a neighbor's door and ask one question: "Would you recommend living here?" Most people will give you an honest answer. Check the building's online reviews across multiple platforms — ApartmentRatings, Yelp, Google Maps. Look for patterns across multiple reviews, not just one outlier. One bad review about a specific incident is different from ten reviews all mentioning the same maintenance delay problem.

---

Applying for an apartment as a student: what you need

Most landlords require: government ID, Social Security number, bank statements showing sufficient funds, proof of income (pay stubs or offer letter), and references from previous landlords. Students often can't meet income requirements (typically 3x monthly rent in gross income). Having a co-signer (usually a parent) is standard and expected — get them prepared to provide their own income documentation.

---

The roommate search: vetting before you commit

Before signing a lease together, have explicit conversations about: daily schedule (early riser vs. night owl), cleaning standards (how often, how thoroughly), guests and overnight visitors, shared groceries vs. separate food, noise levels during study hours, and what happens if one person wants to leave early. Incompatibility on any of these is manageable with clear agreements; it becomes a serious problem when no one discussed it upfront.

---

Student lease vs. standard lease: know the difference

Some properties near universities offer "student leases" that run August to July to align with academic year. These are often joint leases where all roommates sign the same agreement (joint and several liability applies). Others offer "by-the-bed" leases where each person signs individually — you pay only for your room and aren't liable if a roommate stops paying. By-the-bed leases offer better protection but often come with less choice in roommates.

---

After the decision: documenting your new home

Move-in documentation is the single most important thing you can do in your first 48 hours. Photograph every room from multiple angles. Open every cabinet and photograph inside. Test every outlet. Run every faucet. Document any existing damage (scratches, stains, missing fixtures). Send all documentation to the landlord by email — this creates a timestamped record that proves pre-existing conditions existed before you arrived.
