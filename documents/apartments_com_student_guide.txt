Source: Apartments.com Blog — Tips for Renting Your First Apartment Off Campus

---

Before you start touring, set your budget in concrete numbers. A common rule is to spend no more than 30% of your gross monthly income on rent. For students, calculate based on your take-home pay from part-time work plus any predictable financial aid. Don't forget that rent isn't your only housing cost — add utilities, internet, renter's insurance, and parking to your monthly estimate.

---

Apartment hunting checklist: Before you sign anything, verify these items in person. Check water pressure in the shower and sink. Run the garbage disposal. Open and close every window — stuck windows are a fire safety issue. Check that every outlet has power (bring a phone charger). Test the stove and oven. Look under sinks for signs of water damage or mold. Flush the toilet and verify it refills properly.

---

Utilities to always clarify: Not all apartment listings are transparent about utilities. Always ask specifically whether these are included or excluded from rent: electricity, gas/heat, water, sewer, trash collection, internet, parking, laundry, and common-area maintenance fees (in condos). "Some utilities included" in a listing means almost nothing — get the specific list in writing.

---

What to look for in a lease before signing: Read the entire lease, not just the rent amount. Key sections to review: lease term and end date, rent amount and due date, late fee amount and grace period, utility responsibilities, guest policies (some leases limit overnight guests), pet policies even if you don't have a pet, subletting rules, early termination clause, rules for renewal and rent increases, and conditions under which the landlord can enter.

---

Application fees: Most landlords charge an application fee ($25-75) to run a credit and background check. This fee is typically nonrefundable even if you're rejected. Don't apply to five apartments at once unless you're prepared to lose $200 in fees. Prioritize and apply to your top two choices first.

---

Renters insurance is not optional: Your landlord's property insurance covers the building's structure, not your belongings. If there's a fire, a burst pipe, or a break-in, you get nothing for your laptop, TV, or clothes without renters insurance. Policies typically cost $10-20/month and can be bundled with auto insurance for additional savings. Some landlords now require proof of coverage before move-in.

---

Move-in inspection: On move-in day, do a room-by-room inspection and document everything with dated photos and video. Note any pre-existing damage — scratches on floors, stains on carpet, chips in counters, broken blinds. Send the documentation to your landlord by email within 24-48 hours. If a dispute arises later, you have timestamped evidence of what was already there.

---

Security deposit protection: After you move out, your landlord must return your deposit within the state-mandated timeframe (usually 14-30 days) with an itemized statement of any deductions. Keep copies of all maintenance requests you submitted and compare them against any claimed damage. If you believe deductions are improper, you can dispute them in small claims court.

---

Finding roommates: Sharing an apartment significantly reduces per-person costs. For a $2,000/month two-bedroom, each roommate pays $1,000 instead of $2,000. Beyond cost, compatibility matters: discuss sleep schedules, cleanliness standards, guest policies, and how shared groceries work before you sign anything together. A short-term conflict with a roommate becomes a long-term problem when you're both locked into the same lease.

---

Proximity vs. cost trade-off: Apartments closest to campus are almost always the most expensive. Define your commute tolerance in terms of time, not distance — 2 miles by bike might take 12 minutes; 2 miles on a slow bus might take 35 minutes. Weigh the rent savings of being 3 miles farther out against the daily time cost and transportation expenses.
