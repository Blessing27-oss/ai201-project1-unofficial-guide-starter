Source: Nolo.com — Tenant Rights and Responsibilities for Renters

---

Security deposits: Most states cap security deposits at one to two months' rent. Landlords must return the deposit within a legally specified window after move-out (typically 14-30 days depending on the state). They must provide an itemized written statement of any deductions. If they miss the deadline or fail to provide itemization, they may forfeit the right to keep any portion of the deposit.

---

Normal wear and tear vs. damage: Landlords can deduct from a deposit for damage beyond normal wear and tear, but not for ordinary use. Normal wear and tear includes: minor scuffs on walls, small nail holes, carpet wear from regular foot traffic, fading from sunlight. Damage beyond normal wear includes: large holes in walls, stains, broken fixtures, burns, or pet damage. Courts consistently rule that cleaning fees charged for a unit left in reasonable condition are improper.

---

Landlord right of entry: Landlords must give advance notice before entering a rental unit for non-emergency reasons. Most states require 24-hour notice. Emergency exceptions (fire, flooding, gas leak) allow entry without notice. A landlord who repeatedly enters without notice may be violating your right to quiet enjoyment, which is a lease term implied by law in most states.

---

Habitability standard: Every rental must meet basic habitability requirements regardless of what the lease says. This includes: working heat during winter, hot and cold running water, functioning smoke and carbon monoxide detectors, no serious pest infestations, structurally sound walls and roof, and working electrical systems. A landlord cannot waive the habitability standard in a lease — any lease clause attempting to do so is unenforceable.

---

Repairs: If you report a needed repair and the landlord fails to fix it within a reasonable time (typically defined by state law), you may have the right to "repair and deduct" — hire a contractor yourself and deduct the cost from rent — or to withhold rent until repairs are made. Always put repair requests in writing (email is fine) and keep a copy.

---

Breaking a lease early: If you break a lease before its end date, you are typically liable for the remaining rent unless your landlord finds a new tenant. Most states require landlords to make a good-faith effort to re-rent (mitigate damages). If the landlord re-rents in two months and you had four months left, you owe two months, not four. Get documentation.

---

Lease renewal and rent increases: When your lease ends, the landlord can raise rent for a new lease period, subject to any local rent control ordinances. They must typically give 30-60 days notice of a rent increase. Month-to-month tenants usually have shorter notice requirements depending on the state.

---

Retaliatory eviction: A landlord cannot legally evict you or raise your rent in retaliation for reporting code violations, organizing with other tenants, or exercising your legal rights. Retaliatory action within 60-90 days of a protected activity is presumed retaliatory in many states, shifting the burden of proof to the landlord.

---

Subletting: Your right to sublet depends entirely on your lease. Most leases either prohibit subletting entirely or require written landlord consent. If your lease is silent on subletting, state law governs — some states allow it by default, others don't. Subletting without permission when the lease prohibits it can result in eviction for both you and your subtenant.

---

Joint and several liability: When multiple tenants co-sign a lease, each is responsible for the entire rent obligation, not just their proportional share. If one roommate stops paying, the landlord can pursue the others for the full amount. This protects landlords but creates real risk for co-tenants. A roommate agreement (separate from the lease) can govern how you split costs internally, but it doesn't affect your obligations to the landlord.
