Source: Zillow Learn — First-Time Renter Tips

---

Understand the total cost of renting before you commit. Monthly rent is only the start. Add up: security deposit (often one month's rent upfront), application fee, first and last month's rent upfront (some landlords require this), moving costs, utility setup fees, and any required furniture if the unit is unfurnished. The out-of-pocket cost before your first month even begins can be two to four times the monthly rent.

---

Questions to ask every landlord before signing: How long has the unit been vacant, and why? What are the average monthly utility costs in this unit? Has the unit had any water damage, mold, or pest issues in the last two years? What is the process for reporting maintenance requests and what's the typical response time? Is there on-site management or maintenance? What happens to the lease if the building changes ownership?

---

Reading the lease — what to look for: Lease end date and renewal terms, late fee structure and grace period, pet policy (even if you don't have pets — it affects future flexibility), guest restrictions (some leases limit how many nights a guest can stay), subletting rules, what happens if you need to break the lease early, rent increase notice requirements, and who is responsible for lawn care, snow removal, and pest control.

---

Inspect for these specific issues during a tour: Water stains on ceilings or walls (indicates past or current leaks). Cracks around windows and doors (drafts, potential moisture entry, heating inefficiency). Mold in bathroom grout or under sinks. Condition of kitchen appliances — open the oven, run the dishwasher cycle, check under the refrigerator. Outlet placement and quantity — do you have enough for your devices? Is there a dedicated circuit for high-draw appliances? Cell signal in the unit — dead zones can be a real issue in older buildings.

---

Lease red flags: Any clause that waives your right to proper notice before landlord entry. A lease that makes tenants responsible for major structural or HVAC repairs. Unusually long list of move-out cleaning fees specified upfront (often a sign of a landlord who charges for normal wear and tear). No clause specifying timeframe for security deposit return. Any requirement to pay cash only with no written receipt system.

---

When to walk away from an apartment: The landlord can't explain why the previous tenant left. Current tenants (if you can ask them) describe chronic maintenance delays. The unit has visible signs of water damage the landlord dismisses as "already fixed." The landlord refuses to put any agreements in writing or insists everything will be handled verbally. Your gut tells you something is wrong — trust that instinct.

---

After you move in: Change your address with the post office, your bank, and any government accounts (student loans, insurance). Set up a file or folder (paper or digital) where you keep your lease, move-in photos, all correspondence with your landlord, and receipts for any repairs or replacements you make. If a dispute arises later, organized documentation is your best defense.

---

When utilities are not included: Budget realistically. Electricity for a one-bedroom apartment typically runs $60-120/month depending on season and climate. Gas heat in a cold climate can add $100-200/month in winter. Internet runs $50-80/month for a standard plan. Water and trash are sometimes included; ask specifically. Total utility cost for a student apartment commonly runs $150-300/month on top of rent.
