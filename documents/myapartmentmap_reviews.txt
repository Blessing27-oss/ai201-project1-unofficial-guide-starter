The Palms Student Housing — 3.5/5
Location is unbeatable — literally across the street from the engineering building. The units are small but functional. What I didn't expect: the walls are extremely thin. I could hear my upstairs neighbor's alarm clock every morning. If noise sensitivity is a concern, ask to see a unit that isn't on the ground floor or directly below another unit. Parking is $95/month extra. Management is professional and responds within a day or two.

---

Birchwood Apartments — 4/5
Best-kept secret near campus. A 15-minute bike ride from the main buildings, but the lower rent more than offsets the commute time. Two-bedrooms here run $1,100/month ($550/person with a roommate) vs. $1,600-1,800/month for equivalent units closer to campus. Management is a local company (not a national property management chain) and actually answers the phone. Heat is included; electricity is not and runs about $70/month for my unit.

---

University Heights Complex — 1/5
I cannot stress this enough: do not rent here. The building has chronic cockroach issues. Management sends exterminators who treat only surface areas; the infestation in the walls was never addressed. When I moved out, I reported the issue to the city housing authority, and the building had over 30 outstanding code violations on file. Before renting anywhere, check your city's housing code violation database. Many cities have this information publicly searchable online.

---

Creekside Student Apartments — 4/5
The good: in-unit washer/dryer, great natural light, responsive maintenance, and the package system actually works. The less-good: no AC in units, which is brutal in August and September. The landlord says "we've never installed AC and the old buildings can't support it" which may be true but isn't what you want to hear during a heat wave. Check for AC before you commit, especially if you're arriving in late summer.

---

The Arbor — 3/5
Fine overall. My main complaint is the lease renewal process. In March, they sent a renewal offer with a 7% rent increase and a 5-day deadline to sign. Five days to decide whether to re-sign a year-long lease or find a new apartment is not enough time. I pushed back and they extended the deadline to 14 days, which helped. Know your rights: most states require 30-60 days notice for rent increases, and landlords typically cannot enforce a 5-day deadline for lease renewal decisions.

---

Campus Edge Housing — 2/5
The listing photos are about 5 years old. The units look nothing like what's advertised — the "modern kitchen" in the photos has been replaced by the original fixtures which are cracked and stained. The listing shows hardwood floors; the unit has linoleum. This is frustrating but technically legal as long as the unit is habitable. Lesson: always tour in person before signing. Never sign based on photos alone, especially for student housing near universities where landlords can count on high turnover.

---

The Landmark Apartments — 5/5
Genuinely excellent. The property manager lives on-site, which makes a huge difference in responsiveness. When my bathroom sink drain clogged at 10pm, I texted the property number and had someone there the next morning at 8am. Deposit return process was seamless — they emailed me an itemized move-out statement three days after I left, no deductions, full refund in my account eight days later. I've referred four friends here.

---

Pinewood Court — 3/5
The building is older but well-maintained. Pros: included heat (rare in this price range), quiet building, real storage unit in the basement, building manager available 6 days a week. Cons: the coin laundry is expensive ($2.75/wash, $2.00/dry) and often has machines out of service. The parking lot is cramped — 20 spots for 35 units, which creates conflict. If you have a car, the parking situation is genuinely stressful.

---

Madison House — 2/5
The utility cap situation is what I want to warn people about. The lease says "electricity included up to $75/month." In summer the AC bill is $140-180, so I was paying $65-105/month in electricity overages on top of rent. In winter the heat is gas (also included), so winter months were fine. The net effect: this apartment costs $65-105/month more than the listed rent for 4 months of the year. Run the real math before you sign.

---

The Courtyard — 4/5
Good value, responsive management, reasonable lease terms. The standout: the lease renewal process was transparent and fair — 60-day notice of any rent increase, clear terms for how the increase was calculated (CPI-linked), and no pressure tactics. For students, lease transparency matters as much as the unit quality. I knew exactly what I was agreeing to.
